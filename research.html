Research

Historically speaking, my research has been focused in mathematical epidemiology. Compartmental models of infectious disease provide a highly customizable framework which can be used to study disease dynamics. For example, the well-known Kermack-McKendrick (or SIR) model divides the population according to the stage of the disease and then tracks movement between the compartments using a system of nonlinear ordinary differential equations (ODEs).

For my PhD dissertation (defended in September 2017), I worked on a project to model the spread of an infectious disease exhibiting susceptible-infectious-susceptible dynamics through a small metapopulation with delayed dispersal. Using that framework, I built a system of delay differential equations (DDEs) to model the scenario. While similar to ODEs in many ways, DDEs result in strikingly different behavior which may help to better account for certain disease dynamics; however, incorporating DDEs makes the system even more challenging to analyze.

The primary result of my work was to show that the delay parameter does not affect the stability of the disease-free equilibrium. I also used a numerical technique to find thresholds for which the system's qualitative behavior, even though the stability is preserved. Additionally, I explored a simlar problem in a one- and two-dimensional continuous spatial regions. In order to do this, I adapted frameworks given by others for modeling populations, and then added a disease structure to those models.

At the Illinois Mathematics & Science Academy, I oversee two high school students who have developed their own framework for understanding COVID-19, and are working to build deterministic and stochastic models for transmission. I'm excited by their progress, but I'm even more excited to be a part of their discovery process. Moving forward, I hope to build on this work and include more students in mathematical modeling. Even students with only a limited knowledge of calculus can begin to explore the world of mathematical epidemiology; and I would relish in the opportunity to help them start along that path.

In addition to my research in applied mathematics, I am also deeply interested in exploring how we understand and teach mathematics. I have conducted lots on informal research aimed at improving my own instruction; however I hope to continue building my expertise in this realm and eventually conduct more formal studies.
